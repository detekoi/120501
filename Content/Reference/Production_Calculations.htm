Production Calculations

There are two approaches to represent the Production Forecast for a particular well(s).

  1. Formulaic - based on an Initial Production (IP, or Qi) rate, Decline Type (e.g. Linear, Hyperbolic, Exponential, or Harmonic) and Parameters, Onstream Date, and Offstream Date or Rate.
  2. Tabular - a series of entries (typically monthly) that represents the average daily rate for the period.

 

There are a number of Segment Level Modifiers that can be applied to the calculated Production, depending on the particular needs:

  1. WI% - this represents your company's Net Production.
  2. Shrinkage - the Shrinkage % represents 'losses'; which may be due to Derived Product(s) (see below), line losses, or consumption for compression. This factor is represented in Production Calculations via the Raw versus Sales Production definition.
  3. NRI% - this factor (Net after Royalties) combines the Working Interest and Royalty % into a single value. Unlike Royalties or Production Tax, there is no Time basis to this factor. If NRI% is supplied, it takes precedence over any time based Royalty percentages.
  4. Quality - a factor that can adjust the calculated Production in terms of actual heating value (for Gas), or Density (for liquids).

 

In addition to the modifiers listed above, there are a number of options that can impact Production Calculations.

  1. Derived Products - a Production segment can be defined as being Derived from a Primary Product. The Production for this segment will be a ratio (constant or time variant) of the Primary Product's production.
  2. Royalties - every Asset/Product combination can have time series' of Royalty % specified. Three series are available (Low, Medium, High or P10, P50, P90). The names of the individual series is configurable.
  3. Downtime - Unscheduled and/or Scheduled downtime can be defined for the Asset (well). With the exception of the Production Analysis screen, all other Production related calculations (this includes calculations that are based on Production, such as Variable OPEX or Revenue) will be after Downtime.
  4. Curtailment - this is a set of capabilities for modelling the effects of constraints on production at a group level, such as the constraints a facility places on a producing field.

 

And finally, Production based taxes can be configured at a Jurisdiction level to support Economics Calculations.

  1. Production Tax - a certain percentage of a Product's Production is removed as a Tax. Revenue calculations are then performed against the 'removed' Production to determine the corresponding Tax.
  2. Revenue Tax - a dollar / volume tax. This is independent of the corresponding Product Price, but is related to the Production.

 

Quality - Heating Value or Density

A given Production stream can be reported in two ways, depending on the Product Category:

  • Gas - Volumetric or Energy
  • Non-Gas (Liquid or Other) - Volumetric or Mass

Generally, a production segment will be Forecast in volumetric units (Gas in mcf/day, Liquids in bbl/day). However, Price Files (for calculation of Revenue) are often defined in Heating Value (for Gas) or Mass (for Liquids). Each Product has a default Quality (entered in the Configuration), and every Production Segment has data Fields for specifying the particular Quality of the well.

In addition, there are Equivalence Units which allow for cross category reporting - for example Reporting the entire production in terms of a Gas (mcfe) or Liquid (boe). These units will only have a "notional" representation of Quality - for example a GJe unit would be based on a "standard" Heat Content for both Gas and Non-Gas.

 

The Conversion infrastructure is based on the SI system:

For Gas:

  • Volumetric Base - m3 (cubic metres). This is identified in the Configuration as m3 (energy)
  • Energy Base - J (joules)

For Non-Gas:

  • Volumetric Base - m3 (cubic metres). This is identified in the Configuration as m3 (mass)
  • Mass Base - kg (kilograms)

Data Input is only allowed in an appropriate unit - i.e.:

  • A Gas segment can only be defined in either Volumetric (mcf, mmcf, e3m3 (G)), or Energy (btu, mbtu, GJ) basis.
  • A Liquid (Oil, NGLs) segment can only be defined in either Volumetric (bbl,m3, e3m3 (L)), or Mass (T, kT) basis.

 

Equivalence Units (boe, mcfe, GJe, etc.) can be defined in the Configuration to allow conversion between Product Categories (i.e., reporting Gas in a Liquid Equivalence unit). The values entered represent the conversion to boe, i.e.; 1 mcfe = 0.16667 boe.

Production Calculations which report in non-native units (for example when reporting both Gas and Liquid), can only be reported using an Equivalence Unit.

There are several recognized "Standards" which specify Conversion Factors:

  1. For Gas the reference state is dependent on whether the Imperial or SI system is being used as the standard.
  2. The "Base" energy content (BTU/cf) of a Gas has a variety of values.
  3. Similarly, the reference density for Oil can vary.

The only "guidance' is to ensure that a consistent set of inter-conversions are entered.

 

Default Product Qualities are user defined conversion factors:

  1. The Description is a user defined label.
  2. Only one conversion factor can be supplied for each entry: to the base Heating Value definition (J/m3) or base Density definition (kg/m3).
  3. The entries in this table are what are available in the Product Definition table, as well as the individual Production Segments.

 

Individual Products are defined in terms of:

  1. Bookable - whether the Product is to participate in metric (Lifting Cost, F&D) calculations.
  2. Energy - indicates whether the Quality is to be represented in Energy or Mass units.
  3. Units/boe, Unit  - the Base unit for the particular Product and its conversion to the base Equivalence Unit (boe).

 

The Quality conversions represent the Default relationship. For each Product the conversion is represented as a Quality and Quality Unit.

  1. For "Gas" products (the Energy flag will be true), the Quality will be represented in a Heating Value unit defined as per Product Qualities (above).
  2. For "Non-Gas" products (the Energy flag will be false), the Quality will be represented in a Density unit defined as per Product Qualities (above).

 

Each Production segment will use the corresponding Product Default Quality, however, it can be overridden.

 

Forecast Production - Periodic

All Forecast related Production calculations begin with the determination of the Gross Cumulative Production for a particular period. All Production calculations require a Production period (Start Date to End Date), which has generally been specified by the client (Reports, Analysis Screens, Economics, Ranking, etc.) of the calculations.  The client configuration determines the Start and End Date for the various periods being requested (i.e., Monthly, Quarterly, Annual).

  • esi.manage uses the actual number of days in a given period for its calculations. For example, if monthly calculations are requested, January will be based on 31 days of production and February 28 (or 29). A Production Graph will appear somewhat jagged based on the number of days in a particular month.
    • Daily Production Rates are determined by dividing the Cumulative Production by the number of days in the Period. This will smooth out the Production Profile.
  • esi.manage uses the Effective Decline rate formulation of the various Formulaic Decline methods. If your source data is represented as a Nominal Decline, it must be converted to the Effective Decline form.
    • The Decline Interval can be Daily, Weekly, Monthly, Quarterly or Annual. For Production calculations these are converted to a Daily decline rate.
    • Monthly decline intervals are converted based on 30.4375 days/month
    • Quarterly decline intervals are based on 91.3125 days/quarter
    • Annual decline intervals are based on 365.25 days/year.
  • Production Declines from the first day.
  • Tabular Production is held within esi.manage as the Average Daily Production for the period. The cumulative Production is determined by multiplying this value by the number of days in the calculation period.

 

The Production Segment Onstream Date can occur on anywhere in the month. For example, if the Forecast OnStream date is May 25, 2011, the Cumulative Production for May will be based on 7 days (May 25 to May 31).

Production can be calculated up to the Offstream 'point'. This is determined from the calculated end date of a formulaic curve, or the end of entered tabular data.

The Total Cumulative Production for a particular segment is based on the period from the Onstream Date to the Offstream Date. This is the value that will be used to initialize the Gross Technical Reserves amount.

 

Production Downtime

Production Downtime can be expressed in two forms:

  1. Unscheduled - this is a % that represents the anticipated service factor for a particular 'well', expressed as the % of time that the well will be non-producing. For example, a well that has historically produced 75% of the time will have an unscheduled downtime % of 25.
  2. Scheduled - this is used to reflect 'known' downtime periods at some time in the future. Each Scheduled downtime entry has a Start and End Date, and a Downtime % for that period. The Downtime % represents the anticipated reduction - for example if a Facility will be running at 60% capacity for a particular period, the corresponding Scheduled Downtime will be 40%.

Multiple overlapping downtime entries are applied in a sequential order, by default, resulting in a multiplicative result. Unscheduled downtime is applied first, followed by scheduled downtime. In this manner, 50% unscheduled downtime and 50% scheduled downtime in the same period will result in 25% production (100*50% = 50%, then 50*50% = 25%)

Production models may assume that product is 'lost' during downtime, as opposed to an extension of the life of a well. To support such models, esi.manage allows the user to discard downtime production. This option can be toggled within the Configuration perspective (System ->Miscellaneous->Site-specific features>Nonstandard Downtime). Enabling this feature will also calculate downtime in an additive manner (ie. 50% scheduled and 50% unscheduled downtime will have zero production in that period).

Beyond these points, Forecast Production values used or displayed (with one exception) within esi.manage are After Downtime has been applied. The Production Analysis view will display Forecast values with and without Downtime applied.

Production Actuals are a result of any real Downtime, the configured Downtime percentages are not applied to Actuals.

 

Production Definitions

The following table shows how the various Modifiers described above are applied to calculated Forecast Production (after Downtime).

 

With the exception of Royalties (which utilize a Time Series), all other modifiers are single values that are applied to the calculated Forecast Amount.

  1. Note that Actuals (Field Estimates, Actuals and Financial Actuals) only occur after a Well is producing.
  2. Actuals have 0 Risk (POS is 100%), are presumed to be Shrunk (no Shrinkage will be applied), and reflect any actual Down Time.
  3. Actuals are presumed to reflect the Royalties or NRI%, and contain the corresponding Revenue generated from the Production.
    • NRI%, Royalties and Price Files will not be applied to 'Actuals' production data.

In terms of the data entered for these modifiers (and their impact on the reported production):

  • WI% is applied directly. A Net number will be the Gross Production x the WI%.
  • Risk is derived from dependencies in the Project. A Risked production amount will be the Gross (or Net) Production x NPS%.  If Production is not dependent on another measure, you can enter an AF% to get the NPS% for the production segments %.
  • Shrinkage is accounted for with the Sales definitions; which will be the Production Amount (Gross or Net, Risked or Unrisked) x (1 - Shrinkage).
  • NRI% is applied directly, however, it is presumed to include the WI% as part of its value. If NRI% has been supplied for a Production Segment, the WI% will not be applied to calculate Net Risked after Royalty. Risk and Shrinkage will be applied first.
  • Royalties will be applied to the Net Risked Shrunk value for a given reporting period. As Royalties are configured as a time series, the appropriate Royalty Rate for each of the reporting periods will be applied in the form of Net Risked Sales Production x (1 - Royalty Rate).

 

Production Types

Production segments can also contain 'Actuals' (Field Estimates, Actuals and Financial Actuals) data. esi.manage treats the values preferentially:

Financial Actuals are presumed to be the most accurate, followed by Actuals and then Field Estimates.

As indicated above, from a calculation perspective Actuals have no Risk (POS = 100%) and they are Shrunk (Shrinkage = 0).

As an example, consider a variance Report where the Production Type is Actuals. If the Risked Sales Production Definition is compared to Unrisked Raw, the variance will be 0.

 

Each Production Type can be used explicitly for calculations; i.e., Report on Production Actuals. In this scenario, the only factor that will affect the results will be the intersection/overlap between the Data Dates (the periods for each Actuals record) and the Reporting Dates (the Start and End dates for each calculation period).

The precedence of Production Data Types is used in Blended calculations. For a given period, Financial Actuals will be used (if present) first, followed by Actuals, Field Estimates and then Forecast values.

 

Calculation Period versus Data Period

For the following section, the Calculation Period is represented by a Start and End Date. For example, the Production in May, 2011 will be based on May 1, 2011 (Start Date) and May 31, 2011 (End Date).

  1. For Formulaic Production segments, this simply means calculating the Cumulative Production (Onstream Date to End Date) and subtracting the Cumulative Production (Onstream Date to Start Date) which determines the cumulative Production within the Reporting Period.
    • Note that any Down Time percentages are applied to each of the calculation periods.
  2. For Tabular Production (which includes Tabular Forecast) the calculations are based on the relationship between the Reporting Period and the Data Period(s).
  3. For example, the Reporting Period is May 1 to May 31, 2011.
  4. Within that period there are 3 Data periods.
    1. April 28 to May 3 with an average daily rate of 10000.
    2. May 4 to May 25 with an average daily rate of 100.
    3. May 26 to June 4 with an average daily rate of 10.
  5. The calculations for May will be:
    • 3 days (May 1 to May 3) at 10000/day = 30,000.
    • 22 days (May 4 to May 25) at 100/day = 2,200.
    • 6 days (May 26 to May 31) at 10/day = 60.
  6. The total production for May will be 32260.

 

Blended Production Calculations

 There are two methods of Blended Production calculations:

  • Data Driven - this approach will use whatever Data Types exist within a Calculation Period, in order of Preference.
  • Date Driven - this requires Dates (i.e., the Last Actuals Date) to be specified for each Data Type.

Blended Production (as shown in the Production Types figure above) considers Actuals and Field Estimates.

First, we will describe the Date Driven approach. This will require the Last Actuals and Last Field Estimates dates to be identified (either user supplied or initialized from the data within the Budget).

When calculations are being performed:

  1. Only data of Type Actuals will be used for calculation periods up to the Last Actuals Date.
    • If, for a particular Production segment, there are no Actuals, but there are Field Estimates within this period, the reported value will be 0.
  2. Only data of Type Field Estimates will be considered for calculation periods between the Last Actuals and Last Field Estimates Dates.
    • Similarly, if there are Actuals within this Period, they will not be considered.
  3. Only Forecast data will be used after the Last Field Estimates date, regardless of whether there is Actuals or Field Estimates Data.

In addition, the same 'Dates' mechanism described in the Calculation Period section is used when there is an intersection between Calculation periods and Data Type periods.

For example:

  1. The specified Last Field Estimates Date was April 30.
  2. A particular production segment has a Field Estimates entry that covered April 28 to May 5, with an average daily production of 100.
  3. This Field Estimates entry would contribute 300 to the April total, and nothing to May.

With Data Driven Blending, the Data Types are 'lined' up in order of preference (Actuals then Field Estimates then Forecast), and the contributions determined based on the intersection / overlap between Calculation Periods and Data Periods.

 

Derived Products Calculations

A Product (typically NGLs, Condensate, or Water) can be defined in terms of a Primary Product. The Derived Product segment requires an Asset to be identified, which enables the correct Primary Product production segment(s) to be referenced for calculations.

A Derived Product's production profile is defined as a Ratio to the Primary Product:

  • Fixed Ratio - a single value will be used for all time periods.
  • Variable Ratio - a series of ratio's are defined in terms of Offset (days, weeks, months, quarters, years) from the Onstream Date or a specific Calendar Date. The last entry in the table defines the ratio to be used for all time periods beyond that Offset.

In certain applications, it will be desirable to define a chain of derivations. For example:

  1. In a steam injection application, Steam is the Primary Product.
  2. Oil is 'Derived' from the Steam Production.
  3. Gas is then Derived from the Oil

Production calculations for the Derived Product are based on the calculated profile for its Primary Product, multiplied by the appropriate factor. If a Variable Ratio has been specified, the corresponding Production period is used to determine the correct factor to apply.

There are two paths through the Production Calculations depending on how the Primary Segment was defined:

  • Shrinkage = 0 - in this case the Primary Segment's production (IP) has been entered as a Shrunk value.
    • The Derived segment's Production will be the Ratio to the Primary segment's calculated production.
    • The Derived segment's Production will be reported for both a Raw and Sales Production Definition.
  • Shrinkage > 0 - in this case the Primary Segment's production (IP) has been entered as a Raw value.
    • The Derived segment's Production will again be the Ratio to the Primary segment's calculated production.
  • However, what is reported is now dependent on the selected Production Definition and on whether it is a Byproduct or an Associated Product.
    • For a Raw definition - the Primary segment will be Unshrunk and the Derived Byproduct segment's Production will be reported as 0. An example here is NGL derived from Gas.  NGL is in gas phase in the raw gas, and therefore not deemed reportable as a raw product.  To report it AND to report the Raw gas would result in double counting of the NGL molecules.
    • For a Raw definition - the Primary segment will be Unshrunk and the Derived Associated segment's Production will be reported as 0. An example here is water associated with oil.  Water is present and measurable in the raw fluid stream, and therefore deemed reportable as a raw product.
    • For a Sales definition - the Primary segment will be Shrunk (based on the Shrinkage%), and the Derived Byproduct segment's Production will be reported based on the defined Ratio. Using the NGL derived from Gas example, the NGL has now been separated and liquefied (and more importantly the gas volume has been reduced to allow for this extraction). It will be reported based on the defined Ratio.
    • For a Sales definition - the Primary segment will be Shrunk (based on the Shrinkage%), and the Derived Associated Product segment's Production will be reported based on the defined Ratio. This is no different than in the Raw case above.
